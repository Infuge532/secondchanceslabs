# Discovery Questions — Custom Table Maker Website

Categories you already have planned: **Story**, **Process**, **Design/technical preferences**, **Gallery**, **Ecommerce**. Below are the additional angles worth covering in the same session.

## Ideal Client & Audience
- Who is your ideal customer? (private homeowners, interior designers, architects, hospitality/corporate clients?)
- Do you work mostly local, national, or do you ship internationally?
- Do designers or architects ever spec your work into their projects? Should the site speak to that trade audience separately from retail buyers?
- What's the emotional reason people choose a custom table over buying furniture off the shelf?

## Differentiation & Positioning
- What makes your tables different from other high-end or custom furniture makers?
- Is there a signature technique, joinery style, wood species, or finish you're known for?
- Who do you consider your competition, and are there any of their websites you admire or want to avoid looking like?
- Where do you want to sit in the market — "quiet luxury," "heirloom craftsmanship," "modern design-forward," etc.? (This shapes tone, photography style, and copy voice.)

## Materials & Craftsmanship Details
- What wood species, materials, or finishes do you typically offer?
- Do you source materials in any notable way (reclaimed wood, sustainably harvested, specific mills/regions)? Worth a page if so — buyers at this price point often care.
- Do you build made-to-order only, or do you also keep any standard/semi-custom designs available?
- Typical lead time from order to delivery?

## Trust & Social Proof
- Do you have testimonials, reviews, or client quotes we can use?
- Any press, magazine features, awards, or industry recognition?
- Any well-known clients, spaces, or projects you can showcase (with permission)?
- Do you offer any kind of warranty or guarantee on craftsmanship?

## Photography & Brand Assets
- Do you have professional photography of finished pieces, or will that need to be arranged?
- Any behind-the-scenes/process shots (workshop, tools, hands-on building) — these perform very well for "process" storytelling.
- Do you have an existing logo, brand colors, or visual identity, or is this a clean slate?
- Any videos (build process, walkthroughs, testimonials)?

## Business Logistics That Affect Site Structure
- How does a typical client engagement work — consultation call, in-person showroom visit, sketches/approval steps, deposit structure?
- Do you want the site to display pricing/price ranges, or keep everything quote-based ("request a consultation")?
- Do you have a physical showroom or workshop people can visit?
- Do you want an option for virtual consultations or is it strictly in-person/local?

## Site Goals & Success Metrics
- What's the primary goal of the new site — generating qualified leads, supporting sales conversations, pure brand/portfolio presence, or direct ecommerce sales?
- How do most customers currently find you (referral, Instagram, word of mouth, existing site, trade shows)?
- Any existing social media or marketing channels the site should link to or pull content from?

## Content Ownership Going Forward
- Who will maintain/update the site after launch — will he add new pieces to the gallery himself, or will that go through you?
- Does he want a blog/journal section (e.g. "the making of X table," design tips, wood care)? Good for SEO and reinforces expertise.

## Practical/Technical Housekeeping
- Does he have an existing domain and/or website already, or is this greenfield?
- Any existing hosting, email, or business tools (CRM, scheduling) the site needs to integrate with?
- Budget range and timeline expectations for launch?
- Any accessibility, SEO, or analytics requirements/preferences?

---

### Quick tips for the interview itself
- Ask about **materials/process/trust** questions *while* he's mid-story about how the business started — it flows more naturally than a rigid Q&A.
- If he mentions specific past clients or projects, follow up immediately with "do you have photos of that one?" — these tend to become your strongest portfolio pieces.
- Save budget/timeline/logistics questions for the end; they're the least fun to answer and go better once rapport is built.
