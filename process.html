The Process

Weeks of Patience,
Poured Into Every Table.

A slab doesn't become an heirloom overnight. Between the forest and your dining room are six stages — and there are no shortcuts through any of them.

Stage One

Finding the Slab

The more unique the piece, the more it captures the eye. We hunt for walnut with character — quilted grain where a branch compressed the wood, distressed live edges, shapes a lumber mill would call flaws. That's exactly what we're after.

Primarily Kansas walnut · kiln-dried up to two weeks

Stage Two

The Layout

A simple frame is built to the finished dimensions, and the slabs go down on the floor inside it. Then we play — shifting, flipping, and pairing pieces until the composition feels right. Nancy, with her interior-design background, has the final say.

Marked in chalk · cut to fit · approved by Nancy

Stage Three

The Pour

A clear base coat seals the slab to the form so nothing leaks beneath. Then the river gets poured — Total Boat Fathom epoxy, up to two inches deep, tinted with Eye Candy pigments. The shop stays near 70°F so the exothermic cure never runs hot.

3 days to strip the form · 7 days to full cure

Stage Four

CNC Flattening

The cured table travels to a CNC machine that mills the entire surface dead flat — top and bottom. It's why you can run a hand across the seam between wood and epoxy and feel nothing at all.

Machined level · no visible seam

Stage Five

A Week of Sanding

The longest stage. Eight grits, from 60 all the way to 1000, worked across every inch with a Festool sander — top and bottom both. This is where the depth appears: the epoxy turns to glass and the grain lights up underneath it.

60 → 1000 grit · roughly a full week

Stage Six

Finish & Support

A ceramic finish goes on top for a waterproof, furniture-grade surface — and the bottom gets finished too, because unsealed wood drinks moisture like a sponge. Steel C-channels are routed into the underside so the top stays flat through every season.

Ceramic top coat · monthly maintenance spray included

Questions We Hear

Before You Ask

How do I care for an epoxy table?

Easily. Once a month, spray on the maintenance solution we include, spread it around, let it dry, and buff. The ceramic finish is waterproof — everyday cleaning is just a soft cloth.

What if the table is ever damaged?

Repairs are almost always possible, and for most damage we can do the work right in your home at $150 an hour. When we're done, you won't know it was ever there.

Can I pick my own colors?

Yes — that's the point. You choose the epoxy color from Eye Candy's pigment range, the edge profile, and the overall look. We'll guide you through swatches during the design conversation.

Why does it take weeks?

The epoxy alone needs seven days to fully cure, kiln-drying fresh slabs can take two weeks, and sanding through eight grits takes most of a week. Every stage we rush is a flaw you'd find later — so we don't rush any of them.
